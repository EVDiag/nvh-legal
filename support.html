NVH Source Locator — Support

Help, frequently asked questions, and how to reach us.

NVH Source Locator is a professional measurement tool for locating noise, vibration, and harshness (NVH) sources using time-difference-of-arrival (TDOA) calculations. This page covers the most common questions. If you don't find your answer here, please email us — we read every message.

About the App

NVH Source Locator helps engineers, technicians, and researchers pinpoint the origin of noise and vibration in materials and structures. By placing two or more sensors and measuring the time differences of signal arrivals, the App calculates the source location with precision suitable for diagnostic work in automotive, industrial, and structural applications.

The App is available on both iOS (iPhone, iPad) and Android devices, and supports 30 languages.

Getting Started

  1. Select a measurement type from the home screen (2-sensor, 3-sensor, 3D, etc.) based on how many sensors you have available.
  2. Choose your material from the built-in database, or use a custom calibrated value if you have measured the sound speed in your specific material.
  3. Enter the time differences measured between sensors using the +/- buttons or keyboard input.
  4. Place your sensors on the visual grid by tapping the positions on the diagram.
  5. Take a photo of the measurement area and annotate it with sensor positions for your report.
  6. View, share, or print the calculated source location along with all measurement details.

My measurement results don't match what I expected.

Common causes of unexpected results:

  • Material selection — the sound speed varies significantly between materials. Verify you've selected the correct material from the database or calibrated a custom value.
  • Sensor positioning accuracy — small errors in sensor placement positions translate directly to errors in the calculated source location.
  • Time difference measurement precision — your measurement equipment's resolution affects the calculation accuracy.
  • Surface conditions and signal reflections — complex geometries can cause secondary signal paths.

If you're confident in your inputs but still see unexpected results, please email us with details — we're happy to help diagnose.
